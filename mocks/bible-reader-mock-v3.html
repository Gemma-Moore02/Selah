John 4 · Selected Passage
Matthew Henry 1662–1714

Christ's coming into Samaria, and his resting at Jacob's well, was not accidental but providential. He who wearied himself with a journey did so that he might have opportunity to do good — revealing the condescending grace with which he sought the lost.

The woman of Samaria is met here not as a representative of respectability, but of need. Her coming at the sixth hour, the heat of the day, rather than in the cool of the morning with the other women, suggests an outcast. And it is precisely to such that Christ makes himself known.

The request for water is the point of entry — as with Elijah and the widow, as with Abraham's servant and Rebekah at a well. John is building his narrative on the deep grammar of the Old Testament, where a well is always a place of covenant encounter.

On Verse 10 — Living Water

The gift of God here is Christ himself, or rather the Spirit which he gives. The living water is distinguished from standing or stagnant water — it moves, it rises, it sustains. The contrast with the well is deliberate: Jacob's well required labor and a vessel; Christ's gift requires neither.

That it becomes in him a spring is remarkable — not merely a supply from outside, but an indwelling principle of spiritual life. Augustine observed that this water does not satisfy thirst and then cease; it transforms the thirsty into a fountain.

🗺 Geography
The Route Through Samaria
Most Jews traveling between Judea and Galilee crossed the Jordan to avoid Samaria. John's "had to pass through" implies divine necessity, not geography.
⛏ Archaeology
Jacob's Well — Confirmed Site
A well at Tell Balata (ancient Shechem/Sychar) has been venerated as Jacob's Well since at least the 4th century. Excavations confirm it is approximately 32 meters deep, consistent with the woman's remark.
📜 Manuscripts
John 4 in the Papyri
Papyrus 66 (𝔓⁶⁶, c. 200 AD) and Papyrus 75 (𝔓⁷⁵) both preserve John 4, making this among the best-attested chapters in early NT manuscripts.
📖 Historical Context
Jews and Samaritans
The enmity between Jews and Samaritans dates to the Assyrian resettlement of 722 BC. By the first century, the hostility was so severe that many Jews would not eat food handled by Samaritans.

Samaria · Jesus's Journey
John 4:4 — Geographic Context
John 4:4
Why Samaria?

The phrase "had to pass through" (ἔδει) indicates divine necessity in John's Gospel — not geographical convenience. Most Jewish travelers crossed the Jordan to bypass Samaria.

Sychar is identified with modern Askar, near Shechem (Nablus). The well itself remains accessible today inside a Greek Orthodox church.

The journey from Jerusalem to Galilee through Samaria was approximately 70 miles — a 3-day walk.

⛏ Archaeology
Jacob's Well — Confirmed Site
John 4:5–6 · Archaeological Evidence

The well at Tell Balata (biblical Shechem, identified with Sychar) has been continuously venerated as Jacob's Well since at least the 4th century AD, when the pilgrim Egeria described visiting it. A church was built over it by Constantine's direction.

"The well is there to this day… and its water is as cold and clear as it was when the patriarch dug it." — Egeria, Itinerarium, c. 381 AD

Archaeological excavations confirm a well approximately 32 meters deep, cut through solid limestone. The depth is consistent with the woman's observation that Jesus had "nothing to draw with, and the well is deep" (v. 11). Modern measurements put it at 31.5–32 m.

The site is located within the area of ancient Shechem, at the foot of Mount Gerizim — the sacred mountain of the Samaritans, which explains the woman's question about the proper place to worship in vv. 20–21.

The field adjacent to the well is identified in Genesis 33:19 as land purchased by Jacob, later given to Joseph (Gen. 48:22). John's narrative is geographically precise in a way consistent with first-hand knowledge of the region.
